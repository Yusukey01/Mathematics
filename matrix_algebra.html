Diagonal Matrix

A diagonal matrix is (n \times n\) square matrix whose entries outside the main diagonal are all zero. \[ AB = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\ & 4\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 6 & 12 \\ 8 & 15 & 24 \\ 14 & 24 & 36\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ BA = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\ & 4\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 12 & 15 & 18 \\ 28 & 32 & 36\\ \end{bmatrix} \] As you can see the right multiplication by the diagonal matirx \(B\) is product of each "column" of \(A\) and corresponding diagonal entry of \(B\) and on the other hand, the left multiplication by \(B\) affects each "row" of \(A\).

A diagonal matrix with 1's on the main diagonal is called an identity matrix denoted by \(I_n\). Remember, in the matrix multiplication, \(AB \neq BA\) in general but clearly you can see \(AI = IA\) and the resulting matix is just the original \(A\). For example, \[ AI = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\ & 1\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\ & 1\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9\\ \end{bmatrix}. = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9\\ \end{bmatrix} \]
