Normal Distribution

A random variable \(X\) has a normal(Gaussian) distribution with parameters \(\mu \) and \(\sigma^2\) if its p.d.f. is given by \[ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}}e^{\frac{-(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \qquad x \in \mathbb{R} \] where \(\mu \in (-\infty, \infty)\) and \(\sigma > 0\) and it is denoted as \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\).


The above figure shows the normal p.d.f. curve. \(\mu\) is the center, and \(\sigma\) is the distance from the center to the inflection point of the curve. Thus, \[ \mathbb{E }[X] = \mu \qquad \text{Ver }(X) = \sigma^2. \]
The most pupular normal distribution is the one with zero mean and unit variance. We call it the standard normal distribution denoted by \[ Z \sim N(0, 1). \] Any normally distributed random variable can be transformed to a standard normal random variable. If \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), then \[ Z = \frac{X- \mu}{\sigma}\sim N(0, 1). \] This process is called standardizing. Also, the p.d.f. of \(Z\) is denoted by \[ \phi (z) = f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{\frac{-z^2}{2}} \qquad z \in \mathbb{R} \] and c.d.f. of \(Z\) is denoted by \[ \Phi (z) = P(Z \leq z) = \int_{-\infty} ^z \phi (u)du. \]
