Linear Approximations

Linear approximation refers to the process of approximation a function (\f(x)\) near a point \(x_o\) using a linear function. It is used to simplify complex functions locally. \[ L(x) = f(x_o) + f'(x_o)(x -x_o) \] where \(f'(x_o) = \lim_{x \to x_o} \frac{f(x)-f(x_o)}{x-x_o}\) is derivative at \(x_o\).
\(L(x)\) is called the linearization of \(f\) at \(x_o\).

If \(y = f(x)\), where \(f\) is a differentiable function, then the differential \(dx\) is an independent variable and the differential \(dy\) is defined as \[ dy = f'(x)dx. \tag{1} \] Also, if \(dx \neq 0\), then \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = f'(x). \] This equation exactly represents the ratio of differentials is equal to change of rate of \(f\)(=derivative of \(f\)).

Since we have learned linear algebra, you might notice that the differential notation is more flexible than other representations. Now we can consider \(x\) as a vector \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n \) or even a matrix \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\).
Differentials are infinitely small change in some varying quantity. In practice, \(dx\) and \(dy\) are arbitrary small mumbers for numerical computations. \[ (y - y_0) \approx f'(x_o)(x-x_o) \Longrightarrow dy = f(x_o + dx) -f(x_o) = f'(x_o)dx \Longrightarrow f(x_o + dx) = f(x_o) + f'(x_o)dx. \] Now we have more general definition of derivative: \[ df = f(x + dx) - f(x) = f'(x)dx. \] where \(df\) is the amount of change of output, \(dx\) is the amount of change of output, and we can consider \(f'(x)\) as a linear operator acting on \(dx\).
