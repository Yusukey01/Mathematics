Linear Approximations

Linear approximation refers to the process of approximation a function (\f(x)\) near a point \(x_o\) using a linear function. It is used to simplify complex functions locally. \[ L(x) = f(x_o) + f'(x_o)(x -x_o) \approx f(x) \] where \(f'(x_o) = \lim_{x \to x_o} \frac{f(x)-f(x_o)}{x-x_o}\) is derivative at \(x_o\).

\(L(x)\) is called the linearization of \(f\) at \(x_o\). the graph of \(L\) is the tangent line at \((x_o, f(x_o))\). In the end, the linear approximation is the core of differentiation and the ideas behind linear approximations are differentials.

Differentials

Differentials are infinitely small change in some varying quantity. If \(y = f(x)\), where \(f\) is a differentiable function, then the differential \(dx\) is an independent variable and the differential \(dy\) is defined as \[ dy = f'(x)dx. \] In practice, \(dx\) and \(dy\) are arbitrary small mumbers for numerical computations. Also, if \(dx \neq 0\), then \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = f'(x). \] This equation exactly represents the ratio of differentials is equal to change of rate of \(f\)(=derivative of \(f\)).

Since we have learned linear algebra, you might notice that the differential notation is more flexible than other representations of derivative. Now we can consider \(x\) as not only a scalar \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), but also a vector \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \) or even a matrix \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\).
Change in \(y\) corresponding to change in \(x\), or \(dx\) represents \[ \delta y = (y - y_o) = f(x_o + dx) -f(x_o) \] and as \(dx\) becomes smaller, the approximation \[dy = f'(x)dx \approx delta y = f(x_o + dx) -f(x_o)\] becomes more accurate: \[ dy = f'(x_o)dx = f(x_o + dx) -f(x_o) \] Then we obtain the more general definition of derivative of (\f\): \[ df = f(x + dx) - f(x) = f'(x)dx. \] where \(df\) is the amount of change of the output, \(dx\) is the amount of change of the input, and we can consider \(f'(x)\) as a linear operator acting on \(dx\).
