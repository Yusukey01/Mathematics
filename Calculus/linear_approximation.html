Linear Approximations

Linear approximation is a process of approximation a function (\f(x)\) near a point \(x_o\) using a linear function. It simplifies complex functions locally. \[ L(x) = f(x_o) + f'(x_o)(x -x_o) \approx f(x) \tag{1} \] where \(f'(x_o) = \lim_{x \to x_o} \frac{f(x)-f(x_o)}{x-x_o}\) is derivative at \(x_o\).
Equivalently, from equation (1), \[ f(x) - f(x_o) \approx f'(x_o)(x -x_o) \]

\(L(x)\) is called the linearization of \(f\) at \(x_o\). the graph of \(L\) is the tangent line at \((x_o, f(x_o))\). Linear approximations form the foundation of differentiation and provide a local linear model for \(f(x)\). This concept extends naturally to differentials.

Differentials

Differentials describe infinitesimally small changes in quantities. If \(y = f(x)\), where \(f\) differentiable, then the differential \(dx\) is an independent variable and the differential \(dy\) is defined as \[ dy = f'(x)dx. \] In practice, \(dx\) and \(dy\) are arbitrary small mumbers. Also, if \(dx \neq 0\), then we recover the familiar derivative form: \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = f'(x) \] but now the left side represents the ratio of differentials.

The actual change in \(y\) corresponding to change in \(x\)(=\(dx\)) is given by: \[ \delta y = f(x + dx) -f(x). \] As \(dx \to 0\), the approximation improves \[ dy = f'(x)dx \approx \delta y = f(x + dx) -f(x). \] Thus we get the more general definition of derivative of \(f\): \[ df = f(x + dx) - f(x) = f'(x)dx. \] Here, \(df\) is the change in the output, \(dx\) is the change in the input, and we can consider \(f'(x)\) as a linear operator acting on \(dx\).
This highlights the flexibility of differential notation in linear algebra, where derivatives can apply to scalars, vectors or matrices. Now we can consider \(x\) as not only a scalar \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), but also a vector \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \) or even a matrix \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\).
