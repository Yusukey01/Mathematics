Chain Rule

Theorem 1: Chain Rule Suppose \(f(x) = g(h(x))\) where both \(g\) and \(h\) are differentiable. Then \[ df = f'(x)[dx] = g'(h(x))[h'(x)[dx]] \]
Intuitively, if we think about a higher dimensional case, it is clear that the chain rule is not commutative in general. For example, consider \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n, \, h(x) \in \mathbb{R}^p, \, \text{and } g(h(x)) \in \mathbb{R}^m\).
Then the output is the Jacobian matrix \(f'(x) \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\), and to construct this matrix, we need the product of the \(m \times p\) Jacobian matrix \(g'(h(x))\) and the \(p \times n\) Jacobian matrix h'(x).
