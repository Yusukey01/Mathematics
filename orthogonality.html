Inner Product & Orthogonality in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

The inner product of vectors \(u, v \in \mathbb{R}^n \) is a single scalar \[u \cdot v = u^Tv = u_1v_1 + \cdots + u_nv_n.\]
We need the definition of the "length" in higher dimension. The norm of a vector \(v \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is the nonnegative scalar \[ \|v\| = \sqrt{v \cdot v}.\] Also, a vector whose length is 1 is called a unit vector \[u = \frac{v}{\|v\|}\] that maintains its "direction." We call this process normalization, which is useful in many applications such as machine learning. Since we got a "length," now we can define the distance between vectors \(u, v \in \mathbb{R}^n \) as \[dist(u, v) = \|u - v\|. \] Geometrically, \(dist(u, v) \text{ and } dist(u, -v)\) are perpendicular iff they are the same. \[\|u - v\|^2 = (u-v) \cdot (u-v) = u \cdots (u-v) + (-v) \cdot (u - v) = \|u\|^2 - 2u \cdot v + \|v\|^2\] \[\|u - (-v)\|^2 = (u+v) \cdot (u+v) = u \cdots (u+v) + (v) \cdot (u + v) = \|u\|^2 + 2u \cdot v + \|v\|^2\] You might notice that these are close to the Pythagorean theorem:\(\|u +v \|^2 = \|u\|^2 + \|v\|^2 \) iff \(u\) and \(v\) are orthogonal. Two vectors \(u, v \in \mathbb{R}^n \) are orthogonal to each other if their inner product \(u \cdot v =0\). Also, if a vector \(v\) is orthogonal to every vector in \(W \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\), then the vector \(v\) is said to be orthogonal to \(W\). Moreover, the set of all vectors that are orthogonal to \(W\) is called the orthogonal complement of \(W\) denoted by \(W^{\perp}\).
